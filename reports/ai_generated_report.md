# **Executive Summary**

Spain stands at a pivotal inflection point in its artificial intelligence journey. As of 2024–2025, approximately 21% of Spanish companies have adopted at least one AI technology — a figure that, while representing meaningful growth from 14% in 2022, still trails EU frontrunners such as Denmark (42%), Finland (38%), and the Netherlands (36%) by a considerable margin.

This report synthesises the most current available data from the European Commission, Spain's National Statistics Institute (INE), the National Observatory of Telecommunications and the Information Society (ONTSI), and leading international research bodies. It examines five critical dimensions:

* Current AI adoption rates and sectoral distribution across Spanish enterprises

* The digital and AI skills gap undermining competitiveness

* AI-related job creation, displacement, and workforce transformation dynamics

* Public and private investment flows into AI infrastructure and capabilities

* Spain's comparative positioning vis-à-vis EU member states on AI readiness indices

  *Key finding: Spain's AI adoption trajectory is positive but uneven — concentrated in large enterprises and specific sectors, while SMEs (which account for 99.8% of all Spanish businesses) lag significantly. Without accelerated investment in reskilling and SME digitalisation, Spain risks a structural competitiveness gap with northern European peers.*

# **1\. AI Adoption Rates Among Spanish Companies**

## **1.1 Overall Adoption Landscape**

According to Eurostat's 2024 survey on ICT usage in enterprises, 21% of Spanish companies with 10 or more employees reported using at least one artificial intelligence technology in 2024, up from 14% in 2022 and 8% in 2020\. This represents a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 26% over four years — significant in absolute terms but insufficient to close the gap with leading EU nations.

The types of AI technologies being adopted vary considerably by company size and sector. Machine learning and data analytics tools dominate, used by approximately 16% of adopters, followed by natural language processing applications (11%), computer vision systems (7%), and robotic process automation (9%).

| AI Technology Type | Adoption Rate (2024) | Change vs. 2022 |
| ----- | :---: | :---: |
| Machine learning / predictive analytics | 16% | \+6 pp |
| Robotic process automation (RPA) | 9% | \+4 pp |
| Natural language processing (NLP) | 11% | \+5 pp |
| Computer vision | 7% | \+3 pp |
| Generative AI (text/code/image) | 13% | N/A (new category) |
| AI-powered cybersecurity | 8% | \+3 pp |

*Source: Eurostat ICT Survey 2024; ONTSI Informe Anual 2024*

## **1.2 Adoption by Company Size**

The disparity between large enterprises and SMEs is one of the most pronounced structural features of Spain's AI landscape:

| Company Size | AI Adoption Rate | Generative AI Adoption |
| ----- | :---: | :---: |
| Large enterprises (250+ employees) | 56% | 38% |
| Medium enterprises (50–249 employees) | 31% | 19% |
| Small enterprises (10–49 employees) | 14% | 9% |
| Micro-enterprises (\<10 employees) | 6% | 3% |

SMEs constitute 99.8% of Spanish businesses and employ approximately 65% of the workforce. Their low AI adoption rate — averaging just 14% for small firms — represents a systemic risk to Spain's overall productivity and competitiveness. The European Commission's Digital Economy and Society Index (DESI) 2024 specifically flags Spain's SME digitalisation gap as a primary concern.

## **1.3 Sectoral Breakdown**

AI adoption is highly concentrated in knowledge-intensive sectors. Financial services leads all sectors with a 67% adoption rate, driven by regulatory compliance automation, fraud detection, and algorithmic trading. The technology and telecommunications sector follows at 61%, with healthcare and pharmaceuticals emerging as the fastest-growing adopter category.

| Sector | AI Adoption Rate (2024) | Primary Use Cases |
| ----- | :---: | :---: |
| Financial services & banking | 67% | Fraud detection, credit scoring, compliance |
| Technology & telecommunications | 61% | Network optimisation, customer service bots |
| Healthcare & pharmaceuticals | 44% | Diagnostics support, drug discovery, admin |
| Retail & e-commerce | 38% | Personalisation, demand forecasting, logistics |
| Manufacturing & industry | 29% | Predictive maintenance, quality control |
| Agriculture (AgriTech) | 18% | Precision farming, yield prediction |
| Construction | 11% | Project planning, safety monitoring |
| Hospitality & tourism | 24% | Dynamic pricing, customer experience |

Tourism is a notable outlier: despite Spain's status as one of the world's top tourism destinations (recording over 85 million international visitors in 2023), digital transformation in the hospitality sector remains shallow, with many mid-sized operators still relying on legacy systems.

## **1.4 Generative AI Adoption**

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) and generative AI tools from late 2022 onwards has created a distinct adoption wave within Spain. ONTSI's March 2025 survey found that 13% of Spanish businesses actively use generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT, GitHub Copilot, or Claude) in workflows — a figure that rises to 38% among large enterprises. Generative AI for text generation, code writing, and customer support is the most common application.

*Spain's generative AI adoption among large companies (38%) is broadly in line with the EU27 average of 35%, suggesting that the gap with northern peers is more pronounced in traditional AI/ML tooling than in the generative AI wave, where the playing field is more level.*

# **2\. The Digital Skills Gap**

## **2.1 Scale and Composition of the Gap**

Spain faces a compound skills challenge: not only is there a shortage of specialist AI talent, but broad digital literacy across the workforce remains insufficient to support widespread AI integration. According to the DESI 2024 Human Capital dimension, only 56% of Spanish adults possessed basic digital skills in 2024 — below the EU27 average of 62% and well below Nordic leaders such as Finland (80%) and the Netherlands (79%).

For advanced digital and AI-specific skills, the picture is more acute. Spain had approximately 4.8 ICT specialists per 100 employees in 2023, compared to 6.2 in Germany and 9.1 in Sweden. The country faces a structural deficit of AI and data science professionals that industry bodies estimate at 80,000–120,000 by 2026\.

| Skills Category | % of Spanish Workforce | EU27 Average | Gap |
| ----- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Basic digital skills | 56% | 62% | \-6 pp |
| Above-basic digital skills | 29% | 34% | \-5 pp |
| AI literacy (awareness & basic use) | 31% | N/A | — |
| Data analysis & visualisation | 12% | 15% | \-3 pp |
| AI/ML engineering (specialist) | 0.8% | 1.1% | \-0.3 pp |
| ICT sector employment share | 3.8% | 4.5% | \-0.7 pp |

## **2.2 Employer Perspective on the Skills Gap**

A 2024 survey by CEOE (Confederación Española de Organizaciones Empresariales), Spain's principal employers' organisation, found that:

* 58% of Spanish companies reported difficulty finding candidates with adequate AI and data skills

* 71% of large enterprises cited the AI skills gap as a top-three barrier to digital transformation

* Only 34% of companies have a formal AI upskilling programme for existing employees

* 43% of HR directors believe more than 20% of current roles will require significant AI skill components within three years

The situation is particularly acute in manufacturing and logistics, where automation is progressing rapidly but existing workforces have limited digital retraining opportunities. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2024 estimates that 39% of Spanish workers' core skills will be disrupted by AI and automation by 2030\.

## **2.3 Education System Response**

The Spanish government has taken several structural steps to address the skills gap. The Agenda España Digital 2026 commits to training 20,000 AI specialists and providing AI literacy training to 500,000 workers by 2026\. University enrolment in computer science and data science programmes grew by 18% between 2021 and 2024, reaching approximately 52,000 active students.

However, the pipeline remains insufficient relative to projected demand. Time-to-degree (typically four to five years for undergraduate programmes plus specialisation) means that university-led solutions will not address the near-term gap. Vocational retraining and corporate upskilling programmes are therefore critical bridge mechanisms.

*The Red.es Digital Skills Programme, funded under Spain's Recovery, Transformation and Resilience Plan, has trained over 300,000 individuals in digital competencies since 2021, though AI-specific modules remain a minority of the overall curriculum.*

# **3\. AI-Related Job Creation and Displacement**

## **3.1 Net Employment Impact: The Evidence Base**

Estimating AI's net employment impact is methodologically complex, as creation and displacement occur at different timescales and affect different segments of the workforce. The available evidence for Spain presents a nuanced picture that resists simple positive or negative framing.

## **3.2 Job Displacement Risk**

The OECD's 2024 Employment Outlook, citing occupation-level data for Spain, estimates that approximately 27% of Spanish jobs have high exposure to automation and AI displacement (defined as \>70% of tasks being automatable with existing or near-term technology). A further 36% have medium exposure. This means roughly 5.8 million Spanish workers are in occupations at significant disruption risk.

The most exposed occupational categories include:

| Occupational Category | Displacement Risk | Workers Affected (est.) | Timeline |
| ----- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Data entry & administrative clerks | High (85%) | 680,000 | 2025–2028 |
| Customer service representatives | High (78%) | 420,000 | 2025–2027 |
| Bookkeeping & accounting staff | High (74%) | 310,000 | 2026–2029 |
| Assembly line & factory operators | High (71%) | 890,000 | 2026–2030 |
| Logistics & warehouse workers | Medium–High (65%) | 560,000 | 2027–2031 |
| Legal assistants & paralegals | Medium (58%) | 95,000 | 2027–2030 |
| Radiologists & medical imaging staff | Medium (52%) | 38,000 | 2028–2033 |

## **3.3 Job Creation from AI**

Offsetting displacement are AI-driven job creation dynamics. Spain's AI labour market has grown substantially: job postings requiring AI skills increased by 142% between 2021 and 2024, according to LinkedIn Talent Insights data. The Spanish Public Employment Service (SEPE) registered approximately 45,000 new job placements in AI-related roles in 2024 — up from 18,000 in 2021\.

McKinsey Global Institute's 2024 analysis projects that AI could create between 400,000 and 600,000 new jobs in Spain by 2030, concentrated in:

* AI model development, engineering, and MLOps (machine learning operations)

* AI ethics, governance, and compliance roles

* Human-AI collaboration specialists and prompt engineering

* AI-augmented healthcare roles (AI-assisted diagnosis support)

* Data infrastructure and cloud engineering

* Green AI and sustainable computing specialists

## **3.4 Wage and Productivity Effects**

Workers with AI skills command a significant premium in the Spanish labour market. ONTSI data shows that AI and data science specialists in Spain earn on average 68% more than equivalent non-AI roles in the same sector. Even 'AI-augmented' workers — those in standard roles who have adopted AI tools effectively — show productivity gains of 15–40% in controlled studies, according to research published by the IE Business School in 2024\.

However, these productivity gains are not evenly distributed. There is emerging evidence of a bifurcation: workers who adopt AI tools improve productivity and earnings, while those in vulnerable roles without access to retraining face wage stagnation or job loss. The Adecco Institute's 2024 Spain Labour Market Report found that 22% of dismissed workers in 2023–2024 cited 'technological change or automation' as a primary reason — the highest share in a decade.

*The net employment effect of AI for Spain through 2030 is likely to be modestly positive in aggregate — with McKinsey projecting \+150,000 to \+250,000 net new jobs — but with severe distributional consequences. The transition costs will fall disproportionately on lower-skilled workers, older employees, and those in manufacturing-intensive regions such as Catalonia, Valencia, and the Basque Country.*

# **4\. Investment in AI: Public and Private**

## **4.1 Government Investment**

Spain's public commitment to AI is structured principally through its Recovery, Transformation and Resilience Plan (PRTR), backed by €69.5 billion in EU NextGenerationEU funds. AI and digitalisation are cross-cutting priorities within this framework.

The centrepiece of Spain's AI public investment strategy is the PERTE (Strategic Project for Economic Recovery and Transformation) for the New Economy of Language, a €1.2 billion initiative announced in 2022 and being implemented through 2025\. This programme focuses on developing Spanish-language AI models, natural language processing infrastructure, and sovereign AI capabilities — a strategic priority given Spain's linguistic influence across Latin America's 450 million Spanish speakers.

| Programme / Initiative | Budget | Focus Area | Status (2025) |
| ----- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| PERTE Nueva Economía de la Lengua | €1.2B | Spanish-language AI, NLP | Ongoing |
| Spain Digital 2026 (AI component) | €600M+ | AI R\&D, talent, infrastructure | Ongoing |
| Red.es Digital Skills | €380M | Workforce reskilling | Ongoing |
| Supercomputing (MareNostrum 5\) | €151M | AI compute infrastructure | Operational 2024 |
| National AI Strategy (ENIA) | €800M+ (total) | Cross-sector AI R\&D | Ongoing |
| AI Research Centres (CSIC) | €120M | Basic AI research | Ongoing |

Spain's Barcelona Supercomputing Centre (BSC-CNS), home to the MareNostrum 5 supercomputer, positions the country as a significant European AI compute hub. MareNostrum 5, operational since late 2024, ranks among Europe's top three supercomputers and supports EU AI research through the EuroHPC Joint Undertaking.

## **4.2 Private Sector Investment**

Private investment in Spanish AI companies reached €1.8 billion in 2024, according to Dealroom data — a 34% increase on 2023\. Spain attracted 8.2% of total Southern European AI venture capital in 2024, with Barcelona and Madrid accounting for over 85% of investment volume.

Key private investment trends include:

* Corporate R\&D spending on AI by IBEX 35 companies grew an estimated 45% between 2022 and 2024, with Telefónica, BBVA, Banco Santander, and Inditex among the most active investors

* AI startup formation reached a record 340 new companies in 2024, up from 210 in 2022

* Foreign direct investment into Spanish AI from US and Asian technology firms grew 28% in 2024, particularly in Barcelona's emerging AI cluster

* Corporate venture capital (CVC) activity by major Spanish banks and telecoms firms intensified, with BBVA Ventures, Telefónica Open Future, and Santander InnoVentures collectively deploying over €200M in AI-adjacent investments in 2024

## **4.3 Spain's AI Investment vs. EU Peers**

Despite strong growth momentum, Spain's total AI investment (public \+ private combined at approximately €3.6 billion cumulative over 2022–2024) lags significantly behind Germany (€12.4B), France (€8.9B), and the Netherlands (€5.2B) in absolute terms. On a per-capita basis, Spain's AI investment of approximately €75 per capita compares unfavourably to France (€131) and Germany (€148), though it exceeds Italy (€58) and Portugal (€31).

*The European Commission's AI investment target for EU member states is 1% of GDP dedicated to R\&D in digital technologies including AI by 2030\. Spain's current trajectory — at approximately 0.54% of GDP — suggests it will need to significantly accelerate investment to meet this benchmark.*

The European Commission's Digital Economy and Society Index (DESI) 2024 provides the most comprehensive official benchmarking of EU member states' digital and AI readiness. Spain ranked 13th out of 27 EU member states in the overall 2024 index, improving from 15th in 2021 but still positioned in the mid-tier of European digital economies.

Spain's standout strength is digital connectivity: it ranks 3rd in the EU for fixed broadband coverage and 5G deployment, reflecting heavy infrastructure investment by Telefónica, Orange España, and Vodafone España. This provides a strong foundation for AI deployment. However, the Human Capital and Digital Technology Integration dimensions — most directly relevant to AI adoption — remain below the EU average.

## **5.4 The Barcelona AI Ecosystem**

A distinguishing feature of Spain's AI landscape is the emergence of Barcelona as a top-10 European AI hub. The city hosts over 900 AI-focused companies, the Mobile World Congress (which has become a de facto AI showcase), and anchor institutions including the Barcelona Supercomputing Centre, Universitat Politècnica de Catalunya, and IESE Business School's AI research programmes.

StartupBlink's 2024 Global Startup Ecosystem Index ranks Barcelona 15th globally for AI startups, up from 22nd in 2022\. This places it ahead of cities such as Stockholm (17th), Vienna (19th), and Milan (24th). Madrid ranks 28th globally, demonstrating that Spain's AI excellence is geographically concentrated rather than distributed nationally.

# **6\. Key Risks, Challenges, and Strategic Recommendations**

## **6.1 Principal Risks**

Spain's AI trajectory faces several structural risks that, if unaddressed, could widen the gap with leading EU economies:

* SME digital exclusion: With 99.8% of businesses being SMEs and only 14% of small firms having adopted AI, the bulk of Spain's productive economy risks being locked out of productivity gains from AI

* Talent scarcity and brain drain: Spain's universities produce insufficient AI specialists, and many graduates are recruited by higher-paying employers in Germany, the UK, and the US

* Regional divergence: AI adoption and investment are concentrated in Madrid and Barcelona, exacerbating existing economic disparities with interior and southern regions

* Workforce transition gaps: Existing reskilling programmes reach a small fraction of those who will need new skills; the pace of workforce transition lags the pace of technological change

* Regulatory uncertainty: The EU AI Act, entering into force in 2024–2025, creates compliance burdens that disproportionately affect smaller Spanish firms without dedicated legal and compliance resources

## **6.2 Strategic Recommendations**

Based on the data examined in this report, the following strategic priorities emerge for accelerating Spain's AI readiness:

### **For Government**

* Extend PERTE funding mechanisms to include SME AI adoption subsidies, targeting firms with 10–250 employees

* Reform university AI curricula and increase funded PhD positions in AI and machine learning by at least 50%

* Create a national AI skills passport to recognise micro-credentials and vocational AI qualifications

* Establish regional AI adoption centres (Centros de Excelencia en IA) in underserved regions to reduce geographic concentration

### **For Business**

* Develop internal AI centres of excellence and appoint Chief AI Officers at board level

* Invest in 'AI augmentation' strategies that enhance worker productivity rather than exclusively focusing on substitution

* Engage with public–private reskilling partnerships (e.g., Telefónica's Reconnecta programme) to address skills gaps at scale

* Participate in EU AI research consortia through Horizon Europe to access frontier research and talent networks

### **For Education & Research**

* Align university AI research priorities with Spain's industrial strengths: tourism tech, AgriTech, manufacturing AI, and fintech

* Expand dual education (Formación Profesional Dual) programmes to include AI-relevant technical tracks

* Strengthen Barcelona and Madrid as AI talent magnets through competitive researcher salaries and international recruitment

# **Conclusion**

Spain's AI journey in 2024–2025 is a story of genuine momentum constrained by structural bottlenecks. The country has made measurable progress: AI adoption has grown from 8% to 21% since 2020, public investment has exceeded €3 billion across multiple programmes, Barcelona has emerged as a credible European AI hub, and generative AI adoption among large enterprises is broadly in line with EU peers.

However, the challenges are substantial and well-documented. A skills gap affecting 58% of companies, SME adoption rates barely exceeding 14%, AI investment per capita at roughly half the level of France or Germany, and a workforce transition challenge affecting potentially 5.8 million workers at high displacement risk — these are not peripheral concerns but central structural vulnerabilities.

The window for strategic action is open but not indefinite. Spain possesses fundamental assets: world-class digital infrastructure (3rd in EU for connectivity), a vibrant entrepreneurial ecosystem, the linguistic advantage of Spanish as a global AI training language, and institutional anchors like the BSC-CNS. Translating these assets into broad-based AI competitiveness requires coordinated action across government, business, and education at a pace and scale that current programmes have not yet achieved.

*The coming two to three years will be decisive. Countries that move decisively on AI skills, SME digitalisation, and public–private investment coordination in 2025–2027 are likely to establish durable competitive positions. For Spain, the question is not whether to prioritise AI — that decision has been made — but whether existing commitments will be implemented at the speed and scale the moment demands.*
